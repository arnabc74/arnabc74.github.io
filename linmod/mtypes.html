Different types of mixed effects models

Here we shall see different examples of mixed effects models used in real life scenarios.

Variance Components models

The mixed effects model that we used for the school data in the last page was an example of a variance components model. Here the randomness of the data came partly from an assignable cause (the random effect), and partly from unassignable causes (random error), and we had assumed these two sourses of variation to be independent. Thus, the variance of each observed value could be split up into two components $$ \sigma^2_b + \sigma^2_e. $$ Hence the term "variance components".

We want to model birth in terms of the sire effect. This means we want to know how much contribution does the father have on the weight of a new born calf. Obviously, it is of little use to come up with a statement like: The effect of that black ox with a white patch over the right eye is 2.34 kg. The fathers are chosen randomly. All that we care about is "How much variability do the fathers contribute?" 
cow = read.table('cow.txt',head=T)
cow$sire = factor(cow$sire)
lme(wt ~ 1, random = ~ 1| sire, dat)

A model with interaction

Sometimes it may happen that a fixed effect has an interaction with a random effect. Then we take the interaction itself to be a random effect (just as sum of a constant and a random variable is itself a random variable). Here is an example.

An experiment, described in Milliken and Johnson (1992) was conducted by a company to compare between the performances of 3 different brands of machines when operated by the company's own personnel. 6 employees were selected at random and each of them had to operate each machine 3 different times. The ultimate aim is to model the score in terms of machine and operator effects (ignoring time). We have already seen this problem earlier. What we did not mention then was the fact that the 6 operators were chosen randomly. So the operator effect should be random, while the machine effect is fixed. Now certain operators may feel more comfortable with certain machines. So we cannot ignore the possibility of a machine$\times$operator interaction. Since this involves the random operator, the interaction effect must also be random.

The model is: $$ y_{ijk} = \mu + \alpha_i + b_j + c_{ij} + \epsilon_{ijk}, $$ where the fixed $\alpha_i$'s denote the machine effect, the random $b_j$'s the operator effect, and the $c_{ij}$'s the random interactions. We assume $b_j$'s to be iid $N(0,\sigma^2_b)$ and $c_{ij}$'s to be iid $N(0,\sigma^2_c).$ These are independent and also independent of the errors $\epsilon_{ijk}$'s which are iid $N(0,\sigma^2_e).$

In R: 
library(nlme)
dat = read.table('machine.txt', head=T)
dat$machine = factor(dat$machine)
dat$operator = factor(dat$operator)
fit=lme(time~machine, random=~(1+machine)|operator, data=dat,correlation=corAR1())
Notice how we have specified the random effects. Everything with subscript $j$ is random. We have just factored this $j$ out to rewrite $b_j + c_{ij}$ as $(b+c_i)_j.$ The $b$ is just the intercept and $c_i$ is the machine effect (for that particular operator).

Of particular interest are the parts shown in red. In fact, this model has allowed for the two random effects to be correlated! Actually, this out shows that lme allows a covariance structure for the random effects. Indeed, if you look up the help of the lme function, you'll find an argument called correlation: 
Usage:

     lme(fixed, data, random, correlation, weights, subset, method,
         na.action, control, contrasts = NULL, keep.data = TRUE)
This argument proves very useful in practice. It is because of this that we are not using the more modern lme4 package (which does not allow any choice of correlation structures).
