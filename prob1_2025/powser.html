Power series

Qe shall learn about a concept called power series in this page. Here is the definition.

Definition: Power series By a power series (centred at 0) we mean an infinite series of the form $$f(t) = a_0+a_1t+a_2t^2+\cdots$$, where $a_i$'s are real numbers, and $t$ is a real variable.
Clearly a power series must converge at $t=0$. For what other values will it converge. As you can guess, the answer depends on the $a_i$'s. It is possible to choose $a_i$'s in such a way that the power series fails to converge for all nonzero values of $t$. At the other extreme, it is also possible to choose $a_i$'s in a way so that we have convergence for all values of $t$.

But whatever the $a_i$'s may be, the following theorem must hold.

Theorem For every power series there is a number $R\geq 0$ such that the power series converges $\forall t\in (-R, R)$ and fails to converge outside $[-R,R]$.
Two points to note:
  • If $R=0$, then $(-R,R) = \phi$.
  • The theorem is silent about the behaviour at $t=-R$ and $t=R$. Indeed, nothing conclusive may be said in general at those points. All the possibilities are possible. (Yes, I know it sounds like a bad pun!)
The $R$ is obviously unique for a given power series. It is called the radius of convergence of the pws.

A power series behaves very nicely in $(-R,R)$ as shown in the following theorem.

Theorem Let $f(t) = \sum a_n t^n$ be a power series with radius of convergence $R>0$. Then $\forall t\in (-R,R)$
  1. $f(t)$ converges absolutely
  2. f(t) is differentiable the derivative may be computed term by term i.e., $f'(t) = \sum_1^\infty n a_n t^{n-1}.$ This new power series also converges everywhere in $(-R,R)$ (may have even larger radius of convergence).
By using the second property repeatedly, you may differentiate a power series as many time as you wish inside $(-R,R)$.
