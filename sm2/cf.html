Characteristic function

Characteristic function (CF) is a useful tool from probability theory to deal with distributions.
Definition: For any distribution on ${\mathbb R}$, its characteristic function is defined as $$ \phi(t) = E(e^{it X}), t\in{\mathbb R}, $$ where $X$ is any random variable with that distribution.
Notice that the CF of a distribution is a complex valued function defined over entire ${\mathbb R}.$

The two most important facts that we shall use here are the following.
Theorem
  • Every distribution over ${\mathbb R}$ has a CF (i.e., the expectation in the above definition exists finitely).
  • The CF characterises the distribution, i.e., two distributions are the same if and only if their CFs are the same.
It is the latter result that earns characteristic function its name.

Why CF is "good"

CF is a function associated with a distribution. We already know of other functions associated with distributions:
  1. probability mass function (PMF)
  2. probability density function (PDF)
  3. (cumulative) distribution function (CDF)
However, CF is "better" than these in the following two senses:
  • CF exists for all distributions, while PMF and PDF do not.
  • CDF does exist for all distributions, but if $X$ and Y are independent with known CDFs, there is no simple formula to express the CDF of $X+Y$ in terms of them. But it is quite simple to express the CF of $X+Y$ in terms of those of $X$ and $Y.$ (how?)

Moment generating function (MGF)

It is something very similar to CF.
Definition: For any distribution on ${\mathbb R}$, its moment generating function is defined as $$ M(t) = E(e^{t X}), \text{when its exists,} $$ where $X$ is any random variable with that distribution.
Despite the superficial similarity of this with CFs, the MGF is much more ill-behaved (owing to the fact that $e^{tx}$ is an unbounded function though $e^{itx}$ is bounded. In particular, it may not exist for all $t\in{\mathbb R}$ for all distributions. Also, two distinct distributions may have the same MGF (though such examples are rather hard to find). Our textbook disregards this point, and (wrongly) uses MGFs to characterise distributions. We shall rectify the mistake by replacing MGFs with CFs. The math will remain almost the same.

Using CF

The first step to use CF, is to remember the CFs of the standard distributons. This wikipedia page privides a such a list. Eventually, you will need to memorise at least pasrt of this list. For now, we shall need only the CF of the $N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ distributon: $$ \phi(t) = e^{it \mu-\frac 12\sigma^2 t^2}. $$ Remember the form well: inside the exponent you have a quadratic in $t$ where the constant is zero, the linear term is purely imaginary, anf the quadratic terms has negative coefficient. Any such CF must correspond to a Gaussian distributon, the parameters of which may be read off from the coefficients.
Theorem If $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ and $a,b\in{\mathbb R}$ with $a\neq 0,$ then $aX+b\sim N(a \mu+b,a^2 \sigma^2).$

Proof: The CF of $aX+b$ is $$ e^{it(aX+b)} = e^{itb}E(e^{itaX} = e^{itb} \phi(at) = e^{itb} e^{ita \mu-\frac 12 \sigma^2 (at)^2} = e^{it(a \mu+b) -\frac 12 a^2\sigma^2 t^2}, $$ which we immediately recognise as the CF of $N(a \mu+b, a^2 \sigma^2).$

The characterising property of CFs now completes the proof. [QED]

Theorem If $X_1,...,X_n$ are independent and $X_i\sim N(\mu_i,\sigma_i^2)$, and $a_1,...,a_n\in{\mathbb R},$ then $$ \sum_1^n a_i X_i\sim N\left(\sum a_i \mu_i, \sum a_i^2 \sigma^2_i\right). $$

Proof: Direct, using CF. [QED]

Theorem If $X_1,...,X_n\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$, then $\bar X\sim N\left(\mu,\frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right).$

Proof:Special case of the last theorem.[QED]
