Test of hypotheses

You can get the basics from my videos (here and here) or by reading section 7.1 of the Ramachandran and Tsokos book.

Here I shall point out two things that are generally overlooked or glossed over in many places.

Which came first: hen or egg?

Type I error is the error committed when you reject a correct $H_0.$ Most books present it as the definition of type I error. In fact, this is the definition of $H_0.$ The definition of type I error is: the more serious of the two errors. The judgement of "seriousness" comes from domain knowledge. Then, for this serious error, the rejected hypothesis is called $H_0,$ the null hypothesis.

In textbook problems, however, the domain knowledge is carefully left out. So you have no way of judging the seriousness. Instead, the two hypotheses are magically given the labels $H_0$ and $H_1$. You must understand that in the real world, the two hypotheses do not come equipped with such labels. It is you, the statistician, who labels them, and it is based on the relative seriousness of the two errors.

Null hypothesis is our pet hypothesis

If the eveidence in the data set is inconclusive, then we prefer to accept $H_0$ as we want to avoid type I error more than type II error. So acceptance of $H_0$ does not mean we have strong evidence for $H_0$. It means that there is not strong evidence against it. However, rejecting $H_0$ means we have strong evidence against $H_0$ (or, equivalently strong evidence for $H_1$). You must keep this in mind when you report you conclusion in plain language:
  • For accepting $H_0$ you write: At 5\% (or whatever you have chosen) level of significance, the data do not have significant evidence against ... (your $H_0$ in plain language).
  • For accepting $H_0$ you write: At 5\% (or whatever you have chosen) level of significance, the data have significant evidence that ... (your $H_1$ in plain language).
