Quadratic forms

Degrees of freedom

Consider a random vector $\v X\in{\mathbb R}^n,$ where the $n$ components are independent $N(0,1)$ random variables. Then its squared length, i.e., $\|\v X\|^2$ is a random variable whose distribution depends on the dimension $n.$ In this context, the dimension is a parameter of the distribution, and this parameter is called the degrees of freedom. The distribution is called the $\chi^2$-distribution with $n$ degrees of freedom, and written $\chi^2_{(n)}.$

Now let's take some subspace $V\leq {\mathbb R}^n $ of dimension $m,$ say. Project $\v X$ (orthogonally) onto $V.$ Let the projection be $\v Y.$ Then $\v Y$ still has $n$ components but can move only inside $V,$ a space of dimension only $m.$ Then it may be proved that $\|\v Y\|^2$ has a $\chi^2_{(m)}$ distribution. We loosely say that $\|\v Y\|^2$ has $m$ degrees of freedom.

So far there is no mention of quadratic forms. Now they will come into play.

We may write $\|\v X\|^2$ as $\v X'\v X = \v X' I_n \v X,$ a quadratic form. It had $n$ degrees of freedom which also happened to be the rank of $I_n.$ Now $\v Y = P\v X,$ where $P$ is the projection matrix onto $V.$ Then $\|\v Y\|^2 = \v Y'\v Y = \v X'P'P\v X = \v X' P\v X,$ since $P$ is symmetric and idempotent. Notice that the degrees of freedom is again given by the rank of $P$ (which is the dimension of $V$).

Hence for a symmetric idempotent matrix $A$ we say (loosely) that $\v X' A \v X$ has $r(A)$ degrees of freedom. This usage is only for symmetric, idempotent matrices. Also, for any quadratic form the matrix is always considered to be symmetric. So in your example $k=\frac 32.$

Are there cubic or higher forms?

Yes, there are, but they are not usually called by these names. Instead, first quadratic forms are generalised to bilinear forms:
A quadratic form is a function $f(\v x) = \v x' A\v x.$ If you allow the two difeent vectors on the two side, you get a bilinear form, which is a function like $f(\v x, \v y) = \v x'A\v y.$ Here the matrix $A$ may be any square matrix.
A bilinear map is linear in each of its arguments separately (i.e., when the other arguments are help fixed). Next, bilinear maps are generalised to multilinear maps $f(\v x, \v y, \v z,...),$ which are linear in each of its arguments separately.

Are there nonhomogeneous quadratic forms?

Yes and no. No because the expression $\v x' A\v x$ has exactly two components per term. However, you can accommodate nonhomogeneous quadratic forms indirectly. Consider $2x^2-4x + 4.$ Think of it as $2x^2-4xy + 4y^2,$ but with $y=1.$ Then it is $$ \left[\begin{array}{ccccccccccc}x & 1 \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{ccccccccccc}2 & -2\\-2 & 4 \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{ccccccccccc}x\\1 \end{array}\right]. $$ The conic-section formula is one application of this.
