Joint distribution

Just as we had encountered joint distribution while learning about discrete random variables, we have the concept of joint distribution for absolutely continuous distributions as well.

Definition: Let $X,Y$ be jointly distributed random variables. We say that they are jointly absolutely continuous if there is some $f:{\mathbb R}^2\rightarrow[0,\infty)$ such that $$\forall a \leq b, c \leq d~~P\big( (X,Y)\in[a,b]\times[c,d] \big) = \int_a^b \int_c^d f(x,y)\, dxdy.$$ Any such $f$ is called a joint PDF of $(X,Y).$
Note that if $X$ and $Y$ are jointly distributed discrete random variables, then immediatly we are assured of having their joint PMF. But not so in case of absolutely continuous distributions. Even if $X$ and $Y$ are each absolutely continuous, still $(X,Y)$ may fail to be jointly absolutely continuous.

EXAMPLE 1: Suppose $X\sim Unif(0,1)$ and $Y = X.$ Then show that $(X,Y)$ is not jointly absolutely continuous.

SOLUTION: Here the CDF of $(X,Y)$ is $$ F(x,y)=P(X\leq x,\, Y\leq y) = P(X\leq\min\{x,y\}) = \left\{\begin{array}{ll}0&\text{if }\min\{x,y\}<0\\ 1&\text{if }0\leq \min\{x,y\} < 1\\ 1&\text{if }\min\{x,y\} \geq 1\\\end{array}\right.. $$ Hence, if $(X,Y)$ is jointly absolutely continuous, then a joint density would be given by $f(x,y)$, where $$f(x,y) = \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x\partial y} F(x,y).$$ This forces $f(x,y)\equiv 0,$ which is not a PDF. â– 

However, if $(X,Y)$ is jointly absolutely continuous, then both $X$ and $Y$ must also be absolutely continuous.

Conditional distribution

So far the absolutely continuous case closely mimics the discrete case, with integration replacing summation. But we cannot follow the same path for conditional distribution. If $(X,Y)$ are jointly discrete then we defined the conditional PMF of $X$ given $Y=y$ as $x\mapsto P(X=x|Y=y) = \frac{P(x=x,Y=y}{P(Y=y)}.$

So for the jointly absolutely continuous case, we define the conditional PDF of $X$ given $Y=y$ as $f(x,y)/f_Y(y).$ There are, however, a couple of points here:
  1. It is possible to have $f_Y(y)=0.$ Then we define the conditional density as 0.
  2. Unlike conditional PMF, the conditional PDF is not a conditional probability, since $P(Y=y)=0.$
  3. $\int_a^bf_{X|Y}(x,y)\, dx$ does not give $P(X\in [a,b]|Y=y),$ as $P(Y=y)=0.$
  4. However, $\int_c^d \int_a^bf_{X|Y}(x,y)\, dxdy$ does indeed give $P(X\in [a,b], Y\in[c,d]).$
It is this last step (which is the analog of total probability) that justifies the definition of conditional PDF.

Other than this difference, the rest follows as in the discrete case. We have the concepts of conditional expectation, conditional variance etc as usual.

Definition: If $(X,Y)$ is jointly absolutely continuous, then $E(X|Y=y) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty f_{X|Y}(x,y)\, dx$ and $$V(X|Y=y) = E((X-E(X|Y=y))^2|Y=y).$$
The tower property also works as before.
